Learned

High-Level Source Concepts

  • The central problem framing is invisible coordination work: remembering, noticing, deciding, reminding, and following through often become more burdensome than the visible chore itself.
  • Household work is presented as a lifecycle, not a single task. A responsibility can include recognizing the need, deciding the approach, preparing, executing, monitoring, and adapting.
  • Fairness depends less on equal task counts and more on whether each person has clear authority, context, accountability, and time that is treated as valuable.
  • Ownership works best when one person can carry a responsibility end-to-end for a defined period, with shared standards agreed in advance.
  • Shared standards should be explicit enough to reduce rework, resentment, hidden supervision, and "I thought you meant..." loops.
  • The source repeatedly warns against scorekeeping, blame, and using reassignment conversations as a way to dump work.
  • Review moments are part of the system. Responsibilities should be revisited when seasons, capacity, work schedules, family needs, or standards change.
  • Personal time, identity, rest, and self-directed pursuits are framed as legitimate household needs, not rewards after all domestic work is finished.
  • Emotional labor includes anticipation, social coordination, relational repair, and remembering what matters to others; a v1 app should make this visible without pathologizing users.
  • The book uses a branded card-game structure and named exercises; those are useful as cautionary inspiration but should not be mirrored directly.

Original Product Implications

  • Model each responsibility as a durable object with owner, expected outcome, cadence, lifecycle notes, and review date, not as a disposable to-do item.
  • Add an ownership mode that distinguishes "helps with" from "accountable for outcome" so shared work does not hide unmanaged coordination.
  • Prompt users to define a practical "done well enough" standard in their own words for each responsibility.
  • Include a calm check-in flow that asks what changed, what is blocked, what should be rebalanced, and what needs a clearer standard.
  • Track changes to ownership and cadence so users can see patterns without turning the app into a points ledger.
  • Treat personal time and recovery as legitimate planning categories, while avoiding branded language from the source.
  • Support seasonal or life-event reviews because fairness changes when constraints change.
  • Let households create their own categories and responsibilities instead of shipping a source-derived deck or taxonomy.
  • Use neutral language focused on clarity, capacity, and follow-through rather than blame, diagnosis, or partner scoring.
  • Design for conversation preparation: surface prompts and summaries that help users talk, but avoid prescriptive therapy-like advice.
  • Make emotional and social coordination representable as work, while letting users choose whether to track it.
  • Keep reallocation actions intentional: ask why a responsibility is changing and whether context/standards are being handed off.

IP and Safety Risks

  • Do not copy the source's card names, suits, card descriptions, exercises, quizzes, examples, glossary definitions, or assessment wording.
  • Avoid a literal card-game UI, the same count of cards, or a category structure that maps closely to the source.
  • Avoid source-branded terminology in the product experience unless later cleared by an IP review.
  • Do not use images or diagrams from the EPUB or related printable-card PDF.
  • Avoid marketing claims that imply affiliation with, endorsement by, or reproduction of the Fair Play method.
  • Keep the app positioned as household organization and relationship support, not therapy, diagnosis, legal advice, financial advice, or crisis support.
  • Be careful with emotionally loaded prompts; the product should encourage reflection and boundaries without directing users to confront a partner in unsafe circumstances.
  • If future agents use templates seeded from reference materials, those templates need separate IP review before implementation.

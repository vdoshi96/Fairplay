# Learned

## Separation of Sources

### User Project Intent from `References/idea.md`

- The intended product is a household teamwork app, not a therapy product,
  generic task manager, or exact clone of any inspiration source.
- The core user is likely a solo starter in a couple or household where one
  person carries more planning, remembering, and coordination than the other
  person sees.
- The proposed app modules are original product directions: a Load Map,
  Shared Radar, guided Check-In, short experiments, Home dashboard, library,
  household/partner invite model, and privacy controls.
- The idea file already proposes a responsive web/PWA MVP with auth,
  household creation, partner invite, free assessment, paid full assessment,
  partner comparison after both complete, Radar comments/buckets, guided
  check-ins, starter experiments, subscription checkout, and basic resources.
- The draft data model centers on households, members, invites,
  responsibility templates, assessment runs/answers, result snapshots, Radar
  items, experiments, check-ins, decisions, milestones, and subscriptions.
- The strongest product principle from the idea file is that the app should
  make hidden pressure visible while helping users approach it as a shared,
  solvable household problem.

### High-Level Product Lessons from the Better Share EPUB

- Mental load is not just visible chores. It includes noticing needs,
  remembering details, evaluating options, tracking timing, anticipating
  impact, and carrying concern between visible moments.
- Unseen work has different textures: cognitive coordination, emotional
  holding, and physical execution. A product should let users name those
  dimensions without turning them into rigid clinical labels.
- The emotional pain often comes from invisibility, dismissal, lack of
  reliability, and repeated failed bids for support, not simply from an uneven
  count of tasks.
- Hidden expectations are a major conflict engine. People bring standards,
  stories, family norms, social messages, and prior experiences into
  household work without always realizing those assumptions are operating.
- Conversations go better when the product helps users slow down, restate what
  they heard, validate the other person's reality, and move from accusation to
  specific requests.
- Habit change requires repetition and structure. New patterns may feel
  mechanical at first; the product should normalize early awkwardness and help
  users repeat small rituals until they become easier.
- Ownership is not the same as being assigned a visible chore. Real ownership
  means taking initiative, understanding context, following through, and
  learning the household standard over time.
- Handoffs need explicit context. When responsibilities shift, the app should
  help users transfer expectations, timing, blockers, support needs, and the
  definition of "good enough."
- Accountability has several layers: each partner's self-regulation, the
  couple's communication norms, and the household system that needs regular
  adjustment.
- Recurring check-ins matter because fairness is dynamic. Schedules, work
  demands, caregiving seasons, health, capacity, and standards change.
- The book's relationship framing repeatedly externalizes the problem: the
  load and the patterns are what the couple is trying to change, not a partner
  as a defective person.
- Safety boundaries matter. Relationship-support prompts should assume only
  relatively safe, non-abusive contexts and should route unsafe situations to
  appropriate support rather than urging confrontation.

## Actionable V1 Product Implications

1. Make the first assessment a calm load snapshot, not a scorecard.
2. Let users tag responsibilities by hidden effort type: planning/remembering,
   emotional holding, and visible execution.
3. Model ownership separately from helping so the app can distinguish "I do a
   piece" from "I carry the outcome."
4. Add a lightweight expectations prompt to each major responsibility: "what
   does done well enough mean in our household?"
5. Build private-to-shared drafting controls for tense thoughts, then help the
   user convert a draft into a concrete request before sharing.
6. Present partner differences as "places to clarify" rather than "who is
   wrong."
7. Make check-ins short, repeatable, and agenda-like: capacity, one priority,
   expectations, commitments, and appreciation.
8. Include a handoff flow for reassigning or sharing responsibilities, with
   context, timing, support needed, and revisit date.
9. Add appreciation and validation moments so the product does not only expose
   imbalance.
10. Support solo onboarding and partner-resistant situations without making
   the app a tool for ambush, surveillance, or partner shaming.
11. Keep educational content practical and short, focused on household
   organization and communication support rather than therapy advice.
12. Build privacy labels into shared spaces from v1: private draft, shared,
   partner-visible, and bring to next check-in.

## Copycat Risks to Avoid

- Do not copy the book's branded SHARE structure, acronym expansion, named
  meeting format, or agenda language.
- Do not copy the book's "get a load of this" exercise format, prompts, or
  reflection questions.
- Do not copy chapter language, examples, quizzes, scripts, conversation
  lines, troubleshooting responses, or source metaphors.
- Do not copy the public Better Share app's exact tab names, board bucket
  labels, assessment wording, checkout positioning, or visible UI copy.
- Do not replicate a chapter-by-chapter product curriculum that maps directly
  to perspective, expectations, ownership, accountability, handoff, and
  meeting modules.
- Avoid using the same named patterns or labels from the book for conflict
  cycles, partner responses, support types, or behavior categories.
- Avoid marketing claims that imply affiliation with Morgan Cutlip, Better
  Share, Thomas Nelson, or any source-derived method.
- Treat cognitive/emotional/physical load as broad common-language dimensions
  and have IP review confirm whether any specific wording is too close to the
  source or public app.
